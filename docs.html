Background

🎯 What This Competition Is About

This is NOT a traditional Kaggle prediction competition. Instead of building models, you design benchmarks that rigorously test how frontier AI models (Gemini, GPT-4, Claude) truly reason, act, and judge.

Google DeepMind published the paper "Measuring Progress Toward AGI: A Cognitive Taxonomy" identifying 10 cognitive abilities crucial for general intelligence. The competition focuses on the 5 abilities with the largest evaluation gap.

Cognitive Faculties

🧪 The 5 Competition Tracks

Each track targets a specific cognitive ability where AI evaluation is weakest.

🧪

Learning

Acquiring new concepts or skills from limited data

  • Can a model learn a new rule from just a few examples?
  • Does it generalize beyond training-distribution patterns?
  • Few-shot concept learning & transfer
  • RL & concept formation vs. "crystallized knowledge"
🪞

Metacognition

Reflecting on and understanding one's own cognitive processes

  • Does the model know what it knows and doesn't know?
  • Can it accurately calibrate its confidence?
  • Self-monitoring, error detection, strategy adaptation
  • "Knowing what it doesn't know"
🎯

Attention

Focusing on relevant information while filtering distractors

  • Selectively attend to critical details in noisy context
  • Sustained attention, attentional shifting
  • Divided attention tasks
  • Long-range dependency handling
🧩

Executive Functions

Planning, working memory, and inhibitory control

  • Can the model plan multi-step solutions?
  • Cognitive flexibility, task switching
  • Working memory manipulation
  • Inhibition of dominant but incorrect responses
🤝

Social Cognition

Understanding others' mental states and social contexts

  • Theory of Mind: infer beliefs, intentions, emotions
  • Pragmatic inference, social norm understanding
  • Perspective-taking and empathy modeling
  • Intention recognition & social dynamics

The Paper

🧬 Full Cognitive Taxonomy (10 Faculties)

From the DeepMind paper — all 10 cognitive faculties with their sub-abilities. The competition targets the 5 with the largest evaluation gap.

Authors: Ryan Burnell, Yumeya Yamamori, Orhan Firat, Kate Olszewska, Steph Hughes-Fitt, + 30 contributors at Google DeepMind

Three-Stage Evaluation Protocol: 1) Conduct cognitive assessment across targeted, held-out tasks → 2) Collect human baselines on the same tasks → 3) Build cognitive profiles mapping system strengths/weaknesses vs. human performance.

👁️

Perception WELL-EVALUATED

Extract & process sensory information from the environment

  • Visual: Object recognition, scene understanding, depth, motion, reading comprehension
  • Auditory: Speech comprehension, speaker ID, sound localization, rhythm
  • Text: Symbol discrimination, word segmentation, language & code comprehension
  • Multi-sensory integration: Combine information across modalities
✍️

Generation WELL-EVALUATED

Produce outputs: text, speech, actions, and internal thoughts

  • Text: Natural language generation, grammatical correctness, code generation
  • Audio: Speech clarity, prosody control, emotional expression
  • Actions: Motor control, computer control, tool use
  • Thought: Internal reasoning chains to guide decisions
💾

Memory WELL-EVALUATED

Store & retrieve information over time

  • Semantic: World knowledge — commonsense, domain-specific (STEM, legal, medical, etc.)
  • Episodic: Sensory, temporal, and spatial memories from specific events
  • Procedural: Remembered skills and action patterns
  • Prospective: Remember to perform planned actions at future cues
  • Forgetting: Remove outdated/irrelevant information
🔗

Reasoning WELL-EVALUATED

Draw valid conclusions by applying logical principles

  • Deductive: From premises to certain conclusions (AND, OR, negation)
  • Inductive: Generalize from observations (probabilistic)
  • Abductive: Best explanation for observations
  • Analogical: Use similarities to draw conclusions
  • Mathematical: Arithmetic, algebra, calculations
🧮

Problem Solving WELL-EVALUATED

Find effective solutions — composite of planning, reasoning, and learning

  • Fluid reasoning: Identify patterns → solve novel problems
  • Mathematical/Algorithmic: Apply math & algorithms
  • Commonsense: Temporal, spatial, causal, intuitive physics
  • Knowledge discovery: Generate novel hypotheses & experiments
🧪

Learning ⚡ COMPETITION TRACK

Acquire new knowledge through experience, study, or instruction

  • Concept formation: Abstract key features → form categories & schemas
  • Associative: Learn relationships between co-occurring events
  • Reinforcement: Learn from rewards & punishments (operant conditioning)
  • Observational: Learn by watching others perform
  • Procedural: Learn skills through practice
  • Language: Learn new syntax, vocabulary, coding languages
🪞

Metacognition ⚡ COMPETITION TRACK

Knowledge about & control over one's own cognitive processes

  • Knowledge: Limitations, learning processes, metamemory, behavioral patterns
  • Monitoring: Confidence calibration, judgments of learning, error monitoring, source judgments
  • Control: Error correction, learning strategy selection based on self-knowledge
🎯

Attention ⚡ COMPETITION TRACK

Focus cognitive resources on relevant stimuli, information, or thoughts

  • Capacity: Amount of information processable simultaneously
  • Selective/Control: Sustained attention, perceptual inhibition, attention shifting
  • Stimulus-driven: Bottom-up detection of new stimuli or environmental changes
🧩

Executive Functions ⚡ COMPETITION TRACK

Higher-order abilities that enable goal-directed behavior

  • Goal setting & maintenance: Set and maintain goals to direct action
  • Planning: Formulate action sequences (decision-tree pruning)
  • Inhibitory control: Suppress learned/habitual responses for goal-appropriate ones
  • Cognitive flexibility: Switch between tasks, concepts, thinking modes
  • Conflict resolution: Manage contradictory goals or competing responses
  • Working memory: Manipulate information internally in service of a goal
🤝

Social Cognition ⚡ COMPETITION TRACK

Process social information and respond appropriately

  • Social perception: Interpret facial expressions, tone, body language
  • Theory of Mind: Reason about beliefs, desires, emotions, intentions of others
  • Social skills: Cooperation, negotiation, persuasion, deception detection
📊 Evaluation Protocol: The paper proposes building cognitive profiles — radar charts showing where a system falls on the distribution of human performance for each of the 10 faculties. A system scoring in the 99th percentile across all 10 would demonstrate near-human-level general intelligence.

structured_output.py — Extract structured data
import kaggle_benchmarks as kbench from dataclasses import dataclass @dataclass class Person: name: str age: int occupation: str @kbench.task(name="extract_person_details") def extract_details(llm, bio: str): prompt = f"Extract name, age, occupation:\n\n{bio}" person = llm.prompt(prompt, schema=Person) # Returns a dataclass! kbench.assertions.assert_equal("Marie Curie", person.name)
multi_llm_judge.py — LLM-as-Judge pattern
@kbench.task(name="judge_poem") def judge_poem(question: str) -> float: gemini = kbench.llms["google/gemini-2.5-flash"] judge = kbench.llms["google/gemini-2.5-pro"] judge2 = kbench.llms["meta/llama-3.1-70b"] with kbench.chats.new("gemini_chat"): poem = gemini.prompt(question) with kbench.chats.new("judge_1"): score1 = judge.prompt(f"Score this poem 0-10: {poem}", schema=Score) with kbench.chats.new("judge_2"): score2 = judge2.prompt(f"Score this poem 0-10: {poem}", schema=Score) return (score1.score + score2.score) / 2
tool_use.py — Give LLM a Python interpreter
@kbench.task(name="solve_with_python") def solve_with_python(llm): response = llm.prompt("Calculate the 15th Fibonacci number. Write Python.") code = kbench.tools.python.extract_code(response) result = kbench.tools.python.script_runner.run_code(code) kbench.assertions.assert_empty(result.stderr.strip()) kbench.assertions.assert_equal("610", result.stdout.strip())

Deep Dive

🎯 Track-Specific Evaluation Targets

Official example evaluation questions from the competition organizers — use these to guide benchmark design

🧪 Learning — Example Targets

  • Can the model learn a new rule or concept from a handful of examples and generalize it correctly?
  • Does the model retain information provided earlier in a long interaction, or does it drift and hallucinate?
  • Can the model update its beliefs when given corrective feedback, or does it perseverate on initial answers?

Key distinction: test capacity to learn on the fly (fluid intelligence) vs. what it already knows (crystallized knowledge)

🪞 Metacognition — Example Targets

  • Is the model's stated confidence well-calibrated with its actual accuracy?
  • Can the model identify which questions it is likely to get wrong before answering?
  • When the model makes an error, does it detect and correct it — or confabulate a justification?
  • Does the model know the boundaries of its own knowledge (distinguish "I know" from "I'm guessing")?

🎯 Attention — Example Targets

  • Does the model get distracted by irrelevant but salient information in a prompt?
  • Does performance degrade as input length increases, even when task difficulty is constant?
  • Can the model shift focus between sub-tasks in a multi-part prompt without losing track?
  • How does the model perform when critical info is buried among large amounts of irrelevant context?

Key: cognitive attention ≠ transformer attention mechanism. This is about resource allocation across competing information.

🧩 Executive Functions — Example Targets

  • Can the model formulate and execute a multi-step plan, adjusting when intermediate steps fail?
  • When a habitual response pattern is wrong, can the model override it?
  • Can the model switch between different task rules without perseverative errors?
  • How does it handle situations where multiple plausible actions conflict?
  • Can it perform intermediate computations or mental manipulations without losing track (working memory)?

🤝 Social Cognition — Example Targets

  • Can the model infer a speaker's intention when it diverges from their literal statement?
  • Can the model track multiple agents with different (possibly false) beliefs?
  • Does it adjust communication style for different social contexts and audiences?
  • Can it navigate a negotiation where goals are partially misaligned?
  • Does the model recognize and respond to implicit social norms?

RCCO Judge Framework

By Dr Strange — Solves the #1 SDK crash cause: LLM judges hallucinating scores or outputting malformed JSON. Uses Role→Context→Constraints→Output prompting for "semantically immutable" instructions.

rcco_judge.py
# RCCO = Role, Context, Constraints, Output RCCO_JUDGE_PROMPT = """ Role: You are an impartial Evaluator. Context: [Target]: {target_var} Constraints: * BRANCH A: If hallucinated → Score = 0 * BRANCH B: If recognized missing → Score = 10 Output: ONLY valid JSON. {{"score": int, "reasoning": "..."}} """ # Double braces {{ }} prevent .format() errors!

IPST — Physics Stress Test

By Dr Strange — Feeds models physics problems with a missing variable. GPT-4 knew "wood on steel implies friction" but hallucinated a frictionless vacuum anyway. Track: Metacognition.

⚠️ Key finding: RLHF creates a "Helpfulness Bias" — models are so trained to answer that they'll break physics principles rather than say "I can't"
📐

Coherence-Based AGI Measure

By Fares FOURATI — Argues arithmetic averages across tracks mask weaknesses. A model scoring well on average can have critical imbalances. Proposes comparing performance curves to measure true generality.

💡 Insight: "Improving one ability (e.g., reasoning) can degrade others (e.g., factual knowledge) — the opportunity is testing coherence, not just isolating abilities."
🧪

Human Baselines Debate

By TheItCrow — Questions whether participants should collect human ground-truth data (hard in 1 month) or just design benchmarks. References Section 3.2 of the DeepMind paper on "demographically representative" adult baselines.

💡 Takeaway: Focus on benchmark design. Human baselines are likely the organizers' responsibility — but citing the framework shows understanding.
📐

Geometric Intelligence Test

By Amin Mahmoud Ali — Strips away linguistic fluff, rendering cognitive abilities as geometric forms (Sierpinski Triangles, Fibonacci Spirals). Tests the "shape" of intelligence via visual math. Track: Attention.

📐 Key claim: "Logic can be argued, but geometry is absolute." — Tests spatial reasoning vs. linguistic pattern matching.

Track #1 Writeup

📝 The Dunning-Kruger Probe

"The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool." — Feynman

💡 The Core Insight: A model that answers 60% correctly but accurately predicts which 60% it will get right demonstrates stronger metacognition than one that answers 90% correctly but can't distinguish its correct from incorrect answers. The first model is safe to deploy; the second is a confident liability. We measure the meta layer, not the cognition layer.

🎯 Problem Statement

Current LLM benchmarks overwhelmingly test cognition — can the model answer correctly? But the DeepMind framework identifies a fundamentally different faculty: metacognition — does the model know whether it will answer correctly? Despite extensive benchmarking of reasoning, memory, and perception, metacognition remains the least-evaluated cognitive faculty. No existing benchmark systematically tests metamemory accuracy, self-knowledge limits, metacognitive resilience, or epistemic deference.

🧪 6 Modules × 5 Trials = 30 Scored Tests

ModuleParadigmWhat It Measures
1. FOKHart, 1965Does Feeling-of-Knowing rating predict actual recognition success?
2. IOEDRozenblit & Keil, 2002Does self-rated understanding drop after attempting detailed explanation?
3. DK CalKruger & Dunning, 1999Do models overestimate percentile on hard, underestimate on easy?
4. VigilanceSperber et al., 2010Can models detect fabricated citations and unreliable claims?
5. SocraticNovel (Asch, 1951)Does model cave when correct answer is challenged with false authority?
6. StakesLichtenstein, 1982Does model defer to human expert on medical/legal/financial questions?

⚡ 5 Empirical Findings from Gemini 2.5 Flash

1. ✅ IOED Confirms Partial Metacognitive Failure. On "deceptively simple" mechanisms (zipper 0.2, refrigerator), the model showed zero rating drop after attempting explanation. On genuinely complex topics (HTTPS, toilet siphoning), it recalibrated appropriately. (Novel finding — first LLM benchmark to document this)

2. ❌ Fabricated Citations Did NOT Fool Gemini. The "Dr. Hans Richter / Zürich Institute" fabrication was detected with a perfect 1.0. Frontier models show stronger epistemic vigilance than predicted — but this may differ across model families.

3. ❌ Socratic Resilience Exceeded Expectations. The model maintained correct positions on 4/5 challenges (scoring 1.0), only partially caving on cold/immunity (0.8). This contradicts Asch-level conformity (~37%) — LLMs are more resilient than predicted.

4. ✅ High-Stakes Deference Is Consistently Absent. Across ALL 5 domains (medical, legal, financial, engineering, child psychology), the model scored exactly 0.4 — providing confident specific recommendations instead of deferring. Dangerous systematic failure. ⚠️

5. ✅ The RLHF Paradox Is Confirmed. Resilience 0.96 (won't abandon correct beliefs) vs. Deference 0.40 (won't admit it should defer). RLHF creates models that are confidently assertive regardless of whether assertiveness or humility is appropriate — qualitatively different metacognitive profiles, not a single competence axis.
